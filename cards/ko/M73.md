## The Matchup
South Africa will arrive at SoFi Stadium having built their entire identity around keeping the ball, and Canada under Jesse Marsch will be perfectly content to let them try. Hugo Broos has fashioned Bafana Bafana in the image of Mamelodi Sundowns: short, high-tempo build-up from the backline, fullbacks in Aubrey Modiba and Khuliso Mudau bombing into advanced positions, and a wide forward line that, when it clicks, offers Lyle Foster multiple angles of service. The problem with constructing a team that presses aggressively man-to-man and sends defenders into the attacking third is that it creates the exact pockets of space behind the defensive line that Canada's 4-4-2 is specifically designed to exploit. When Ismaël Koné or Stephen Eustáquio win the ball in midfield — which Marsch's pressing triggers are calibrated to make happen whenever South Africa cycle laterally — the Canadian forwards do not need a second invitation to run.

At its core, this is a contest between a team that wants to control matches with the ball and a team structured to punish exactly that ambition. Canada's counterattacking threat is real — Jonathan David, the country's all-time leading scorer with 39 international goals, is the kind of finisher who does not need many chances. South Africa need Teboho Mokoena to function as the structural glue that keeps the press from unravelling. If he does, Bafana can pin Canada back. If he doesn't, a Canadian side that can transition in an instant will make them pay on the break. Win or go home, and neither team's path to the Round of 16 has much margin for error.

## Key Duel
Teboho Mokoena against the Canadian midfield double pivot of Ismaël Koné and Stephen Eustáquio is the contest that is likely to determine who books a place in the Round of 16. Mokoena is South Africa's positional anchor, the player whose reading of space allows his teammates to press high without leaving the backline dangerously exposed. Koné and Eustáquio are, by the nature of Marsch's system, asked to do roughly the inverse: disrupt opponents in transition, win the ball, and immediately feed the forwards before defensive shape can be restored. South Africa's build-up depends on Mokoena finding the right pass under pressure; Canada's counterattacking game depends on Koné and Eustáquio winning that ball before he finds it. It is, in the most direct sense, one man's playmaking intelligence against two men's pressing energy. Whoever wins that exchange most often will likely win the match.
