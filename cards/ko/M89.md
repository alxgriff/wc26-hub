## The Matchup
Paraguay arrive at Lincoln Financial Field as the tournament's great overachievers — a team that spent much of qualification below 10th in CONMEBOL before Gustavo Alfaro reordered their house around a 4-4-2 / 4-4-1-1 block sturdy enough to take points off Argentina, Brazil, and Uruguay. That shape bends defensively inward: two compact lines of four, a target man in Antonio Sanabria to relieve pressure up the pitch, and Gustavo Gómez anchoring everything at centre-back. Against a France side that asks its opponents to solve four interchangeable attackers, Paraguay's structural consistency will be tested from the first whistle to whatever final whistle this single-elimination tie produces.

Didier Deschamps does not overcomplicate things. He sets a basic defensive shape, hands the keys to Kylian Mbappé, Michael Olise, Ousmane Dembélé, and Désiré Doué, and lets the chaos unfold. Each of those four can migrate across the front line; each can manufacture a decisive moment without a set play. Behind them, Adrien Rabiot steps forward while Aurélien Tchouaméni covers — a pairing designed to recycle possession and protect the back four without demanding much creativity from themselves.

The central tension is simple arithmetic: Paraguay's 4-4-1-1 is built to deny space in the central corridor, but France's front four live in the half-spaces and wide channels where those midfield lines are hardest to maintain. ## Key Duel
Michael Olise versus the Paraguay right side. Olise arrives off a season in which he accumulated 48 goal involvements across all competitions, and the Champions League semifinal against PSG confirmed he has outgrown the rising-star label. He can operate as a central no. 10 or drift wide, and that positional ambiguity is precisely what Paraguay's defensive structure is least equipped to handle: their lines work best against teams that commit to a channel. Whoever occupies Paraguay's right flank — whether Juan José Cáceres at full-back or a dropping midfielder — will face Olise's ability to cut inside or combine with Théo Hernandez overlapping from the left. Gómez and Alderete are capable aerial defenders, but Olise attacks space rather than contests headers. If Paraguay cannot set their right-side coverage before France get him the ball in transition, it is the most direct route to the most likely scoreline.
