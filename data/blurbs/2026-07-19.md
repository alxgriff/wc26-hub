The group stage is done. All 72 matches have been played, the standings are settled, and the bracket for the knockout rounds is now fixed — which means Sunday offers something the previous five and a half weeks have not: a full day with nothing to resolve. No late equalisers to chase, no goal-difference arithmetic to run at midnight. For the first time since the tournament opened, the World Cup simply pauses and breathes.

It is, in its way, a useful moment. The teams that survived have a day to collect themselves before the round of 32 begins; everyone else has the leisure of the postmortem. The group stage at this scale — 72 matches across nearly six weeks — has a way of making the knockout rounds feel almost abrupt when they arrive. They arrive tomorrow.
